What is Carnatic Music?

Carnatic music is a traditional form of vocal-oriented singing originating from South India. It combines specific rhythms, melodies, and physical methods of keeping time to create full-length songs that usually discuss complex emotions, feelings of devotion, or an admiration of the world. While improvisation plays a huge role in musical practice for Carnatic pieces, there are also a large collection of structural compositions most generally attributed to the composers Purandaradasa, Tyagaraja, Dikshitar, and Sastri.


Stages of Learning:

Sarali Swaras
Sarali Varisai or Sarali Swaras are the first set of exercises that beginner Carnatic vocal musicians are taught. They are a series of rhythmic exercises that help establish note patterns and scales in Carnatic music. These exercises vary note order to build familiarity with different rhythms, pitches, and tonal uses.

Alankarams
Alankarams combines sarali swaras with a measure of time, known as a tāla. These exercises introduce the concept of physically keeping time with each note song through finger patterns and different hand beats. Any tāla can be likened to a metronome, and practicing keeping a tāla alongside singing helps establish speed and controllability of vocal notes. In Carnatic music, speed is measured as the number of notes per tāla beat.

Geethams
Geethams are a simplified version of full-length Carnatic songs, introducing more melodies and adding lyrics to sing using the tonality taught by note patterns in swaras. Musicians in this stage are usually transitioning out of beginner exercises, and a practical tip is to first sing the swaras and then replace them with lyrics. Once musicians grow comfortable with the notes, they start to add in the tāla to control the variability of the song.

Varnams
Starting Varnams is an indication of a transition to an intermediate level of learning Carnatic songs, as it fully establishes melodic frameworks (known as ragams) to sing by. During this stage, a proficiency in note patterns, lyrics, musical rhythm, and advanced tālas is expected. These are usually the first songs at Carnatic music concerts with multiple singers.

Krithis
Krithis are a longer, more comprehensive version of varnams that are commonly sung during Carnatic concerts. Understanding the foundations of krithis indicates mastery over the foundational concepts of Carnatic music and puts musicians into the advanced stages of learning. This is also the stage that directly leads into preparing for longer, solo concerts.

The bulk of the Carnatic songs and verses found today originate from the 15th through 19th century. This was the golden age for Carnatic music, and both composers and vocalists were abundant. Tyagaraja is one of the most famed composers of this time period, heralded as a Saint of Classical music and believed to have composed over 24,000 krithis. For an introduction to Carnatic music, checking out the 800 works that are still around today is a great place to get acquainted with the origins of Carnatic music.
